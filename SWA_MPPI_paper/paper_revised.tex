\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================
Large language models are deployed worldwide but reason about moral dilemmas through a single, predominantly Western lens, and closing this gap usually means fine-tuning, reward modelling, or per-culture labelled data---resources that do not exist at the scale of the world's cultures. We ask whether a frozen model can instead be nudged toward country-specific moral preferences at inference time, using only what cross-cultural surveys already tell us. We propose \textbf{SWA-PTIS} (Socially-Weighted Alignment with Prospect-Theory Importance Sampling), which equips the model with a small panel of culturally-grounded personas derived from World Values Survey data and applies a single KL-regularised importance-sampling correction in logit space whose utility is evaluated by a cooperative Prospect-Theory value function. The update self-attenuates when personas agree---logically producing no correction in the limit of zero disagreement---without requiring an explicit gating rule. We evaluate on MultiTP across 15 countries, 12 native languages, and six moral dimensions, using five open-weight models from 7B to 72B. On the two strong instruction-tuned 70B-class backbones in our panel (Qwen2.5-72B, Llama-3.1-70B), mean Jensen--Shannon distance to country-level human preferences drops by $0.032$ in absolute terms on each model ($-37.6\%$ on Qwen2.5-72B, $-34.7\%$ on Llama-3.1-70B), exceeding every prompting and activation-steering baseline we tried, while smaller 4-bit-quantised models show consistent directional gains. Mechanistic ablations against deterministic shifts and a binary ARGS variant point to one load-bearing ingredient: the Prospect-Theory gain/loss asymmetry operating on KL-regularised perturbations. Code: \texttt{[repository]}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

When a self-driving car must choose between sparing a group of elderly men or a
group of young women, there is no culturally universal answer. The Moral Machine
experiment~\citep{awad2018moral}-40~million judgments from 233~countries-revealed
that such preferences vary profoundly across cultures: East Asian respondents weight
youth more heavily; Southern-hemisphere populations show distinct utilitarian calculi;
Islamic societies prioritise different social roles. Yet the large language models
increasingly enlisted to assist with such decisions encode a single, culturally
monolithic moral stance inherited from their predominantly Western training
data~\citep{henrich2010weirdest}.

The multilingual successor benchmark MultiTP~\citep{jin2025multitp} operationalises
this evidence into country-specific Average Marginal Component Effects (AMCE)
across six moral dimensions and 107 languages, providing a rigorous test bed for
cross-cultural alignment. The verdict is stark: \citet{jin2025multitp} found that
none of 19~contemporary LLMs achieved broadly satisfactory alignment-most exhibit
WEIRD-biased reasoning that departs markedly from non-WEIRD human preferences.

How should this gap be closed? Existing approaches each sacrifice critical properties.
\emph{Fine-tuning}~\citep{bai2022constitutional, ouyang2022instructgpt} demands
per-culture labelled data and modifies weights irreversibly.
\emph{Prompting-based steering} relies on ad-hoc instructions with no empirical
grounding~\citep{jin2025multitp}.
\emph{Activation steering}~\citep{arditi2025refusal, zou2023representation} requires
white-box access and per-model engineering.
\emph{Reward-guided decoding}~\citep{khanov2024args, mudgal2024controlled} needs trained
reward models unavailable for cross-cultural settings.
\emph{Culture-aware adapters}~\citep{li2024culturemanager, yao2024caredio} scale poorly
across 100+ countries. None of these methods can adapt an LLM's moral reasoning to a
target culture \emph{at inference time} without labelled data or weight modification.

We propose \textbf{SWA-PTIS} (Socially-Weighted Alignment with Prospect-Theory
Importance Sampling), built on a simple insight: \emph{disagreement is signal}.
Moral preferences within any society are not monolithic-age cohorts and social
strata exhibit systematically different priorities~\citep{awad2018moral,
kleiman2017learning}. The World Values Survey~\citep{haerpfer2022wvs} provides
empirically calibrated data on these within-country differences across 80+ nations.
When culturally grounded persona agents derived from WVS data \emph{disagree} on a
moral decision, that disagreement reveals the decision is culturally contested-and
can be exploited through importance-sampled inference-time control to
produce a more culturally representative output.

Concretely, SWA-PTIS constructs $N=4$ WVS-grounded personas per country (three
age cohorts plus a utilitarian agent), evaluates all $N+1$ agents in a single batched
forward pass with native-language prompting across 12 languages, and extracts
decision logits. If inter-agent variance exceeds a calibrated threshold~$\tau$, it
samples $K=128$ perturbations and scores each via a Prospect Theory value
function~\citep{kahneman1979prospect} that captures the asymmetric gain-loss
sensitivity characteristic of human moral judgment. The importance-weighted PT-IS
update yields a minimal-KL correction; a positional debiasing step cancels recency
effects~\citep{liu2024lost}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A training-free framework} (\S\ref{sec:method}) that turns
        within-country WVS variation into a usable steering signal: persona
        disagreement gates a Prospect-Theory-weighted IS correction in logit
        space, applied only when the decision is culturally contested.
  \item \textbf{Empirical evidence on six models} (7B--126B; \S\ref{sec:results})
        showing that the largest, cleanest gains come on the well-calibrated
        70B-class backbones, while smaller and stronger models exhibit distinct
        regimes that we report and analyse rather than aggregate.
  \item \textbf{A mechanism analysis backed by a robustness suite}
        (\S\ref{sec:ablation}, Appendices~\ref{app:tau_holdout}--\ref{app:extended}).
        Ablations isolate the utilitarian anchor and Prospect-Theory asymmetry
        as the load-bearing components. Deterministic consensus-shift
        baselines and a Binary-ARGS adaptation then show that WVS weighting
        alone is insufficient and that the Prospect-Theory gain/loss kernel is
        the active ingredient. The remaining sweeps-held-out $\tau$
        calibration, three temperature sweeps, and six dataset preprocessing
        configurations-all stay within the bootstrap noise floor, ruling out
        the obvious confounders.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Cross-cultural moral benchmarks.}
The Moral Machine experiment~\citep{awad2018moral} provides 40M+ cross-cultural moral
judgments. MultiTP~\citep{jin2025multitp} extends this to 107 languages with
country-specific AMCEs-we adopt it as our benchmark and propose an alignment
\emph{intervention} rather than diagnostics alone. Other benchmarks
(ETHICS~\citep{hendrycks2021aligning}, MoralExceptQA~\citep{jin2022exceptions},
MoCa~\citep{nie2023moca}) remain English-centric.
PRISM~\citep{kirk2024prism} and GlobalOpinionQA~\citep{durmus2023global} address
diversity but cover few languages. Recent frameworks
(EvalMORAAL~\citep{aakanksha2024evalmor}, CARB~\citep{myung2024carb}) emphasise
broader-topic evaluations; SWA-PTIS targets binary moral decisions specifically.

\paragraph{Cross-cultural LLM evaluation.}
LLM outputs correlate most strongly with liberal, educated American
respondents~\citep{santurkar2023opinions}. RLHF introduces unintended cultural
impacts~\citep{ryan2024unintended}, and assigning cultural personas can surface
toxic outputs~\citep{deshpande2023toxicity}. This recurring WEIRD bias motivates
SWA-PTIS's calibration objective.

\paragraph{Inference-time alignment.}
Activation steering~\citep{arditi2025refusal, zou2023representation} requires
white-box access. ARGS~\citep{khanov2024args} and controlled
decoding~\citep{mudgal2024controlled} need trained reward models-unavailable for
cross-cultural settings (Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}).
CultureManager~\citep{li2024culturemanager} and CAReDiO~\citep{yao2024caredio}
train culture-specific adapters but require per-culture labelled data and weight
modification, scaling poorly across 100+ countries. To the best of our knowledge,
SWA-PTIS is the first training-free method targeting country-level moral
calibration specifically.
\citet{sorensen2024roadmap} articulate the goal of pluralistic alignment, which
SWA-PTIS operationalises concretely.

\paragraph{Positioning against adjacent inference-time methods.}
Recent work on inference-time alignment falls into several families that we
deliberately do not compete with head-to-head. \emph{DIFFPO}-style
policy-agnostic aligners require an additional learned aligner model on top
of the base LLM, trading deployment cost for flexibility; \emph{SWAI}-style
training-free logit steering targets attribute-level shifts (style,
toxicity) via fixed steering vectors, not country-conditioned moral
preferences; \emph{SLED}-style self-logit evolution is a white-box
factuality method that operates on internal layers. SWA-PTIS is positioned
narrowly within this design space: black-box, single-model, binary
forced-choice moral decisions with country-conditioned persona grounding,
and no extra trained aligner. Relative to structured multi-agent pipelines
such as OG-MAR~\citep{ogmar2024}, SWA-PTIS trades ontology-level reasoning
depth for the much lower deployment cost of one additional batched forward
pass.

\paragraph{Prospect Theory.}
PT-IS is a sampling-based importance-sampled inference-time control
algorithm originally developed for robotics; adapted it
for token-level LLM steering. We use IS in logit space and combine it with a
Prospect-Theory utility~\citep{kahneman1979prospect} to capture the asymmetric
gain/loss sensitivity characteristic of human moral judgment.

\section{Method: SWA-PTIS}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:method:problem}

Let $\mathcal{X}$ be the set of moral dilemma scenarios, each presenting a forced
binary choice via decision tokens \texttt{A}~($a$) and \texttt{B}~($b$).
For target country $c$, let $\mathbf{h}^{(c)} \in \mathbb{R}^6$ be the human AMCE
vector from 40M+ judgments~\citep{awad2018moral}. A frozen LLM $f_\theta$ produces
logits $\mathbf{z}(\mathbf{x}) = [z_a,\, z_b]$ at the decision token position;
the spare probability is $p_{\text{spare}} = \sigma(\Delta / T_{\text{dec}})$
where $\Delta = z_b - z_a$.
Each dimension's alignment is measured via a \textbf{mean preference rate} (MPR):
\begin{equation}
  \hat{m}_d^{(c)} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}_d^{(c)}|}\sum_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{S}_d^{(c)}}
  p_{\text{spare}}(\mathbf{x}),
  \label{eq:mpr}
\end{equation}
following the MultiTP evaluation convention~\citep{jin2025multitp}.
For the five \emph{binary} dimensions (Species, Gender, Age, Fitness, Social
Value), each scenario's grouping indicator takes values $\{0,1\}$; under the
balanced conjoint design~\citep{awad2018moral}, the empirical mean
$\hat{m}_d^{(c)}$ equals the sample analogue of the classical Average Marginal
Component Effect (AMCE), the probability that the ``preferred'' group is
spared~\citep{awad2018moral}.
For \textbf{Utilitarianism}, the treatment variable $n_{\text{diff}}$ (the
numerical life-count difference) is a multi-valued ordered attribute, not a
binary indicator. The proper conjoint AMCE for a continuous treatment is the
regression \emph{slope} $\hat{b}$ from fitting
$p_{\text{spare}} = a + b\,n_{\text{diff}}$---the marginal change per
additional life.
Scenarios with $n_{\text{diff}}=0$ (equal group sizes) are excluded because
they carry no utilitarian signal.
Following the MultiTP LLM-evaluation convention~\citep{jin2025multitp}, we
report as the model's Utilitarianism score the \emph{mean preference rate}
$\hat{m}_{\text{Util}}^{(c)} = \operatorname{mean}(p_{\text{spare}})$ over the
remaining scenarios (Appendix~\ref{app:amce}). We do this for a single
reason: the MultiTP human ground-truth vector to which we compare is itself
computed as a mean preference rate, so an apples-to-apples JSD requires us to
use the same estimator on the model side.
\textbf{We explicitly flag this as a confound}: the MPR mixes two signals that
a slope-based AMCE would separate. Writing
$\hat{m}_{\text{Util}} = \hat{a} + \hat{b}\,\bar{n}_{\text{diff}}$ (where
$\bar{n}_{\text{diff}}$ is the mean life-count difference in the evaluation
pool), the MPR depends on both the intercept $\hat{a}$ (a baseline
``sparing'' preference independent of the utilitarian cue) and the slope
$\hat{b}$ (the actual utilitarian sensitivity). Improvements in MPR are
therefore improvements in their joint, not proofs of improved utilitarian
sensitivity in isolation. Appendix~\ref{app:amce} reports an additional
slope-based AMCE analysis and discusses the remaining confounding.

\textbf{Goal:} Without modifying $\theta$, find a logit correction
$\delta^*(\mathbf{x}, c)$ such that the corrected AMCE vector
$\hat{\mathbf{m}}^{(c)}(\theta, \delta^*)$ is closer to $\mathbf{h}^{(c)}$
under Jensen--Shannon Divergence.

\subsection{Cultural Persona Construction}
\label{sec:method:personas}

For each country $c$, SWA-PTIS constructs $N=4$ persona agents from WVS Wave~7
individual-level microdata~\citep{haerpfer2022wvs}. We extract 8 value dimensions
from specific WVS items: \emph{gender egalitarianism} (Q58P, Q59P, Q60P),
\emph{religious importance} (Q6P), \emph{interpersonal trust} (Q43P),
\emph{moral permissiveness} (Q50, Q52P, Q54P), \emph{work centrality} (Q5P),
\emph{family importance} (Q1P), \emph{personal autonomy} (Q39P), and
\emph{meritocratic orientation} (Q40P). For each country, respondents are
segmented into three age cohorts using birth year and survey year:
\emph{young} (18--35), \emph{middle-aged} (36--55), and \emph{older} ($>$55).
Dimension means are computed per cohort and converted into natural-language
descriptors via fixed thresholds (Appendix~\ref{app:personas}).
A fourth, country-invariant \emph{utilitarian agent} maximises lives saved.
Each persona is a natural-language system prompt:

\begin{quote}
\small\itshape
You are a [age-cohort] adult from [country]. The cultural values typical of your
social group are: [WVS statistics]. When making moral judgments, you tend to [value
description reflecting WVS profile].
\end{quote}

\noindent WVS grounding ensures empirically measured within-country variation rather
than stereotypes~\citep{deshpande2023toxicity}. Concrete persona examples and the
WVS-to-trolley dimension linkage are detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:personas}.

\subsection{Batched Evaluation and Logit Extraction}
\label{sec:method:eval}

All $N+1=5$ input sequences (base + 4 personas) are evaluated in a \emph{single}
batched forward pass:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_{\text{base}},\; \mathbf{z}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{z}_N
  = f_\theta\!\left(\mathbf{x};\; \varnothing,\; \mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_N\right).
  \label{eq:batch}
\end{equation}
Per-category logit temperatures $T_{\text{cat}}$ (Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}) scale
logits before computing decision gaps:
\begin{align}
  \delta_i &= \frac{z_{i,b} - z_{i,a}}{T_{\text{cat}}}, \quad
  \delta_{\text{base}} = \frac{z_{\text{base},b} - z_{\text{base},a}}{T_{\text{cat}}}, \quad
  \bar{\delta} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \delta_i \quad \text{(consensus)},
  \label{eq:consensus} \\
  r_i &= \delta_i - \delta_{\text{base}} \quad \text{(per-agent reward)}.
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{align}
Here $\delta_{\text{base}}$ is the decision gap of the base prompt (no persona).
The reward $r_i$ measures how strongly persona $i$ shifts the model away from
the \emph{base model output}: a large $|r_i|$ indicates that the persona has
identified a direction of moral preference the vanilla model does not exhibit.
We note that $\delta_{\text{base}}$ is not a culturally neutral anchor---RLHF-tuned
base models encode WEIRD-skewed priors~\citep{santurkar2023opinions}---so $r_i$
measures persona deviation from a biased baseline rather than from a neutral
one. This is intentional: SWA-PTIS's goal is to \emph{correct} the base model,
so personas that agree with it ($|r_i| \approx 0$) provide no corrective signal
and are appropriately downweighted.

\subsection{Persona Disagreement Monitoring}
\label{sec:method:tau}

In the current implementation, the IS update runs unconditionally on every scenario.
The per-agent persona variance
\begin{equation}
  \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(\delta_1, \ldots, \delta_N)
  \label{eq:tau}
\end{equation}
is computed and recorded as a diagnostic: it quantifies how much the personas
disagree about the preferred direction for each scenario. A high variance
indicates strong persona conflict (the IS correction draws on a wider spread
of importance weights), while a low variance indicates persona consensus (the
IS correction is small, leaving the result near $\bar{\delta}$).
Adaptive gating---running the IS update only when variance exceeds a calibrated
threshold $\tau^{(c)}$---is a natural extension. A threshold calibrated at the
$65^{\text{th}}$ percentile of variances on $n_{\text{cal}}=50$ held-out
scenarios would target a 35\% intervention rate. We validated this design in
preliminary experiments and found $|\Delta\text{JSD}|=0.0006$ between the
gated and ungated variants across 15 countries (Appendix~\ref{app:tau_holdout}),
confirming that the unconditional IS update is a safe default: the importance-weighted
update self-attenuates when personas agree (the optimal $\delta^*$ collapses
toward zero), making an explicit gate redundant in practice.
We note that with $N=4$ personas, per-scenario variance estimates are inherently
noisy: small-sample estimates with four observations have high relative uncertainty
regardless of the underlying distribution. This noise is compounded by
cross-persona correlation---all four $\delta_i$'s are produced by the same frozen
base model on the same prompt, differing only in the persona prefix, so they share
substantial structural dependence. The self-attenuating property of the IS update
is therefore doubly beneficial: it avoids hard reliance on unreliable per-scenario
variance for gating, and the ungated variant empirically matches the gated one to
within $|\Delta\text{JSD}|=0.0006$ (Appendix~\ref{app:tau_holdout}).

\subsection{Prospect-Theory Weighted Importance Sampling}
\label{sec:method:ptis}

SWA-PTIS draws $K{=}128$ perturbations
$\epsilon_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_k^2)$ and evaluates a Prospect-Theory
welfare score for each candidate $\tilde{\delta}_k = \bar{\delta} + \epsilon_k$.
The noise standard deviation $\sigma_k$ is set \emph{adaptively} per scenario:
$\sigma_k = \operatorname{std}(\delta_1,\ldots,\delta_N)$ (the inter-agent logit
spread), ensuring the perturbation magnitude is commensurate with actual
persona disagreement across models and categories. A fixed fallback
$\sigma_0 = 0.3$ is used when all agents agree exactly
($\sigma_k < 10^{-6}$).

\paragraph{Per-agent gain in logit-gap units.}
For each persona $i$, define the \emph{progress toward persona $i$'s target}
produced by candidate $\tilde{\delta}_k$ as the reduction in logit-gap distance
to the persona's own decision gap:
\begin{equation}
  g_{i,k} \;=\; \bigl|\delta_{\text{base}} - \delta_i\bigr|
              \;-\; \bigl|\tilde{\delta}_k - \delta_i\bigr|.
  \label{eq:agreement}
\end{equation}
This quantity has three properties that make it the correct input to a
Prospect-Theory utility:
(i) It lives in the same \emph{logit-gap space} as $\delta_i$, $\delta_{\text{base}}$
and $\tilde{\delta}_k$---there is no unit mismatch and no logit-gap-squared
artefact.
(ii) It is \emph{bounded}: $|g_{i,k}|\le|\tilde{\delta}_k - \delta_{\text{base}}|$
by the triangle inequality, so it cannot explode when personas agree.
In particular, when all personas collapse to the base
($\delta_i\to\delta_{\text{base}}$) we have $g_{i,k}\to-|\epsilon_k|\le 0$, so
every utility becomes a loss, the importance weights concentrate on
small-$|\epsilon_k|$ samples, and $\delta^{\star}\to 0$---the correct
self-attenuating behaviour at consensus.
(iii) It is \emph{sign-meaningful}: $g_{i,k}{>}0$ iff the candidate moves
strictly closer to persona $i$'s target (a gain), $g_{i,k}{<}0$ iff it moves
away (a loss). This matches the gain/loss framing that Prospect Theory was
built for~\citep{kahneman1979prospect}.

\paragraph{Prospect-Theory value and collective utility.}
We use the canonical Kahneman--Tversky value function
\begin{equation}
  v(x) = \begin{cases}
    x^{\alpha} & x \geq 0,\\
    -\kappa\,|x|^{\beta} & x < 0,
  \end{cases}
  \qquad \alpha=\beta=0.88,\ \kappa=2.25
  \label{eq:prospect}
\end{equation}
applied \emph{per persona, before any averaging}. Averaging $v(g_{i,k})$
across personas (rather than averaging $g_{i,k}$ first and then applying $v$)
is essential: by Jensen's inequality, $\mathbb{E}[v(g)] \neq v(\mathbb{E}[g])$
for the concave-convex kink of $v$, and the former is the only aggregation that
preserves loss aversion at the social level. An ``average gain then evaluate''
scheme would let individual gains and losses cancel linearly before the
$\kappa{=}2.25$ loss-aversion kick in, which is exactly the anomaly Prospect
Theory was introduced to model.

The collective per-sample utility is therefore
\begin{equation}
  U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_k)
  \;=\; (1-\lambda_{\text{coop}})\cdot\underbrace{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} v\!\bigl(g_{i,k}\bigr)}_{\text{mean of individual PT utilities}}
  \;+\; \lambda_{\text{coop}}\cdot\underbrace{v\!\bigl(g_{\bar{\cdot},k}\bigr)}_{\text{consensus-target utility}}
  \;-\; \alpha_{\text{ctl}}\cdot\frac{\epsilon_k^{\,2}}{2\sigma_k^{\,2}},
  \label{eq:util_total}
\end{equation}
where $g_{\bar{\cdot},k} = |\delta_{\text{base}}-\bar{\delta}| - |\tilde{\delta}_k-\bar{\delta}|$
is the progress toward the \emph{consensus} target
$\bar{\delta}=\tfrac{1}{N}\sum_i\delta_i$, $\lambda_{\text{coop}}=0.7$, and
$\alpha_{\text{ctl}}=0.05$. Both Prospect-Theory terms apply $v(\cdot)$ to a
single scalar gain (an individual gain in the first term, a consensus-target
gain in the second), so neither averages inside the nonlinearity.
The cooperation weight $\lambda_{\text{coop}}=0.7$ was selected by grid search
on a 200-scenario validation set (Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}); the high
social weight reflects the task of producing a \emph{culturally representative}
output, which naturally rewards candidates close to the persona consensus.

\paragraph{Quadratic control cost.}
The term $\alpha_{\text{ctl}}\,\epsilon_k^{\,2}/(2\sigma_k^{\,2})$ is a
\emph{quadratic control cost} on the perturbation magnitude, equal (up to an
additive constant) to the negative log-density of the Gaussian prior
$p(\epsilon_k)=\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_k^{\,2})$ at $\epsilon_k$. Under the
softmax-weighted update below, its expectation under the sampling distribution
$q$ recovers $\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,p)$ up to a constant, so it plays the role of
a \emph{distributional} KL regulariser that discourages $q$ from drifting far
from the prior; $\alpha_{\text{ctl}}$ tunes the strength of this trust region.
We deliberately do \emph{not} identify $\epsilon_k^{\,2}/(2\sigma_k^{\,2})$
with a per-sample log importance ratio
$\log\bigl(p(\epsilon_k)/q(\epsilon_k)\bigr)$: for
$p=\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^{\,2})$ and $q=\mathcal{N}(\delta^{\star},\sigma^{\,2})$
that ratio equals $(-2\epsilon\delta^{\star}+(\delta^{\star})^{2})/(2\sigma^{\,2})$,
which depends on $\delta^{\star}$; our term does not. It is the negative
log-prior, nothing more, and we treat it as such.

\paragraph{Importance-weighted update.}
Given the per-sample utilities, we form softmax weights and return the
importance-weighted perturbation:
\begin{equation}
  w_k \;=\; \frac{\exp\!\bigl(U(\epsilon_k)/\eta\bigr)}
                 {\sum_{k'}\exp\!\bigl(U(\epsilon_{k'})/\eta\bigr)},
  \qquad
  \delta^{\star} \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k\,\epsilon_k,
  \label{eq:is_update}
\end{equation}
where $\eta = 0.5$ is the softmax temperature and $T_{\text{dec}} = 0.5$ is the
sigmoid decision temperature. The $\eta$ grid
$\{0.1,0.25,0.5,1.0,2.0\}$ yields a JSD span below $0.005$
(Appendix~\ref{app:is_theory}). The effective sample size
$K_{\text{eff}} = 1/\sum_k w_k^{\,2}$ serves as a per-scenario diagnostic of
the exploration--exploitation balance.
This softmax-weighted expectation is the standard importance-sampling estimator
for a Boltzmann policy under utility $U_{\text{total}}$.

\subsection{Positional Debiasing and Final Prediction}
\label{sec:method:debias}

To cancel recency bias~\citep{liu2024lost}, each scenario is evaluated twice:
once in the original ordering and once with \emph{both} choice labels
(A$\leftrightarrow$B) and group labels (Group~A$\leftrightarrow$Group~B) swapped
in the native language. The full SWA-PTIS pipeline (persona evaluation, reward
computation, IS optimisation) is run independently on each pass, yielding
two optimised decision gaps $\delta_{\text{opt}}^{(\text{orig})}$ and
$\delta_{\text{opt}}^{(\text{swap})}$. The debiased decision gap is then
\begin{equation}
  \delta_{\text{opt}}^{\text{deb}}
  = \frac{\delta_{\text{opt}}^{(\text{orig})} - \delta_{\text{opt}}^{(\text{swap})}}{2}.
  \label{eq:debias}
\end{equation}
Under a \emph{first-order additive} model of positional bias---the model
systematically adding a constant $b$ to the decision logit gap when option A
appears first---the original pass gives $\delta_{\text{opt}}^{(\text{orig})} =
\delta_{\text{true}} + b$ and the swapped pass gives
$\delta_{\text{opt}}^{(\text{swap})} = -\delta_{\text{true}} + b$, so
$\delta_{\text{opt}}^{\text{deb}} = \delta_{\text{true}}$ exactly.
In practice, positional effects in Transformers arise from attention patterns
and may not be perfectly additive at the logit level~\citep{liu2024lost};
the additive model is a standard first-order approximation in the LLM
debiasing literature. We adopt it here and validate it empirically: removing
the two-pass step increases JSD by 12.0\% (ablation row~5, Table~\ref{tab:ablation}),
and the mean cross-country positional-bias estimate of 0.193 logit units
(Appendix~\ref{app:ablation_detail}) is stable across languages, consistent
with an approximately additive and roughly constant shift.
Debiasing at the logit level before applying the sigmoid is critical: averaging
the two \emph{probabilities} after sigmoid would give a biased result due to
sigmoid nonlinearity (i.e.\
$[\sigma(\delta_{\text{true}}+b)+\sigma(\delta_{\text{true}}-b)]/2 \neq
\sigma(\delta_{\text{true}})$ in general).
The final prediction is then
$p_{\text{spare}} = \sigma(\delta_{\text{opt}}^{\text{deb}} / T_{\text{dec}})$
with $T_{\text{dec}} = 0.5$.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:swa} summarises the full procedure; Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}
lists all hyperparameters (validation details in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{SWA-PTIS Inference for Scenario $\mathbf{x}$ in Country $c$}
\label{alg:swa}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Frozen LLM $f_\theta$; personas $\{\mathbf{s}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$;
         threshold $\tau^{(c)}$; hyperparameters $K,\sigma,\lambda_{\text{coop}},\alpha_{\text{ctl}},\eta,T_{\text{dec}}$
\Ensure $p_{\text{spare}} \in [0,1]$
\For{$\text{order} \in \{\text{orig}, \text{swap}\}$}
  \State Batched forward: evaluate $f_\theta$ with base + $N$ personas
  \State Extract logit pairs; compute $\delta_i^{(\text{ord})}$ \hfill\Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:batch}}
\EndFor
\State Average: $\delta_i \leftarrow (\delta_i^{(\text{orig})} - \delta_i^{(\text{swap})})/2$ \hfill\Comment{Debiasing}
\State Compute consensus $\bar{\delta}$, rewards $r_i$, variance $\nu$ \hfill\Comment{Eqs.~\ref{eq:consensus}--\ref{eq:reward}}
\If{$\nu \geq \tau^{(c)}$} \hfill\Comment{Conflict detected}
  \For{$k = 1$ \textbf{to} $K$}
    \State Sample $\epsilon_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$; compute $U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_k)$ \hfill\Comment{Eqs.~\ref{eq:agreement}--\ref{eq:util_total}}
  \EndFor
  \State $\delta^* = \sum_k w_k \epsilon_k$ \hfill\Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:is_update}}
\Else \quad $\delta^* \leftarrow 0$
\EndIf
\State \Return $p_{\text{spare}} = \sigma((\bar{\delta} + \delta^*) / T_{\text{dec}})$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sec:exp:data}

We evaluate on MultiTP~\citep{jin2025multitp}: trolley-problem vignettes in native
languages across six moral dimensions (Table~\ref{tab:categories}). For each of 15
countries, we apply: (i)~deduplication (retain only original-phrasing vignettes),
(ii)~quality filtering (remove Utilitarianism scenarios where both groups have equal
size, as these carry no utilitarian signal), (iii)~category capping (max 80 per
dimension), and (iv)~up-sampling via random oversampling with replacement (min 36
per dimension for stable AMCE estimation), yielding 310--500 scenarios per country
(Appendix~\ref{app:dataset}). The adaptive $\tau^{(c)}$ is calibrated on 50
unlabelled scenarios using only inter-agent variance statistics-never human AMCEs.

\subsection{Models}
\label{sec:exp:models}

Five LLMs: \textbf{Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct}~\citep{qwen2025technical} (Int8),
\textbf{Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct}~\citep{grattafiori2024llama} (FP8), and
\textbf{Qwen2.5-7B/32B-Instruct} and \textbf{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct} (4-bit).
All on a single H100 80GB via Unsloth~\citep{unsloth2024} with greedy decoding.

All experiments use a single seed (42); the $K{=}128$ IS perturbations are drawn once per scenario.

\textbf{Native-language evaluation.} Each country's scenarios are presented
\emph{entirely in the native language}: scenario framing, character descriptions,
lane labels, and closing questions are all translated into 12 languages (zh, ja, ko,
de, fr, pt, ar, vi, hi, ru, es, en). Only the \emph{decision tokens}
(\texttt{A}/\texttt{B}) remain English-these are verified as single-token
entries in each model's vocabulary (Appendix~\ref{app:token_elicitation}). This
design ensures that the moral reasoning is grounded in the target language's
conceptual framing while maintaining a consistent, unambiguous decision interface
across all models and languages.

\subsection{Baselines and metrics}
\label{sec:exp:baselines_metrics}

On Llama-3.1-70B we compare (1)~\textbf{Vanilla} (generic system prompt),
(2)~\textbf{Country-Tailored Prompt (B1)},
(3)~\textbf{WVS Profile Prompting (B2)},
(4)~\textbf{PRISM-Style Prompting (B3)}~\citep{kirk2024prism},
(5)~\textbf{Activation Steering}~\citep{arditi2025refusal} with culturally
contrastive steering vectors (implementation: Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}).
Reward-guided methods (ARGS~\citep{khanov2024args}, controlled decoding~\citep{mudgal2024controlled}) are excluded because they require reward models we do not have for cross-cultural targets (Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}).
As in the MultiTP protocol~\citep{jin2025multitp}, \textbf{JSD} (primary, $\downarrow$) is the Jensen--Shannon distance between model and human AMCE vectors~\citep{lin1991divergence}; each 6-dimensional AMCE vector is converted to a 12-element probability vector by pairing each dimension's preferred/contra proportions (normalised to sum to one) before computing JSD (Appendix~\ref{app:amce}). \textbf{Pearson $r$} ($\uparrow$) correlates the six-dimensional AMCE vectors; \textbf{MAE} ($\downarrow$) is mean absolute AMCE error.
Country means are unweighted across 15 countries; 95\% confidence intervals use bootstrap resampling ($B{=}1000$)~\citep{efron1979bootstrap}.

\subsection{Cross-Model Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:model_summary} summarises six models, but it should be read by \emph{regime}, not by ranking headline percentages.
For the \textbf{strong 70B-class backbones} (Qwen2.5-72B, Llama-3.1-70B)-our intended operating point-vanilla JSD is high enough to leave real room for correction, logits are well-spread, and persona disagreement is informative; on both, mean JSD falls from roughly $0.09$ to roughly $0.05$ (about three centi-units absolute), Pearson $r$ moves into a meaningfully positive range, and this is where we stake our main empirical claim.
\textbf{Small quantised models} (Qwen2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B) start from lower vanilla JSD ($\approx 0.05$ vs.\ $\approx 0.09$); SWA-PTIS improves consistently, but percentage gains sit on a small denominator and are a directional signal rather than a headline.
On \textbf{Qwen2.5-32B} the headline delta is essentially zero: logit concentration at the decision token pushes fixed-$\sigma$ perturbations outside the effective logit range, importance weights go nearly uniform, and the IS update behaves like noise (full diagnostics and adaptive-$\sigma$ analysis: Appendix~\ref{app:adaptive_sigma}), so SWA-PTIS needs a base model whose decision-token logits are not collapsed.

\subsection{Baseline Comparison on Llama-3.1-70B}

SWA-PTIS is the best of the methods we tried on Llama-3.1-70B
(Table~\ref{tab:baselines}), beating the strongest prompting baseline by an
absolute $0.0068$ JSD. Activation steering performs \emph{below} vanilla,
suggesting that contrastive steering vectors interfere with moral reasoning
on this benchmark rather than helping it.

\paragraph{Isolating the active mechanism.}
To check that the gains come from \emph{Prospect-Theory asymmetry} rather than
from any of the other moving parts, we run two families of stripped-down
baselines that share SWA-PTIS's infrastructure (batched multi-agent forward
pass, two-pass debiasing) and differ only in the decoding rule. Deterministic
consensus shifts close most of the gap to SWA-PTIS but never quite catch it.
A Binary-ARGS variant with WVS-salience weighting (no Prospect Theory) is
\emph{worse} than uniform weighting in $12/15$ countries; the same variant
with the Prospect-Theory utility recovers SWA-PTIS in $13/15$ countries. The
combined message is that WVS weighting alone does not help-the asymmetric
gain/loss kernel is the active ingredient. Full tables, baseline definitions,
and per-country numbers are in Appendices~\ref{app:cs_baseline}--\ref{app:args}.

The results establish a clear hierarchy: (i)~the \textbf{utilitarian persona} is most
critical (+43.5\% JSD without it; Pearson $r$ collapses by 0.283), anchoring the
ensemble along the numerosity dimension; (ii)~\textbf{PT-IS} adds meaningful
refinement (+15.9\% JSD); (iii)~\textbf{positional debiasing} corrects substantial
recency bias (+12.0\% JSD; Species preference inflated by 7.7pp without it);
(iv)~\textbf{Prospect Theory} contributes through nonlinear loss-averse weighting
(+4.5\% JSD vs.\ linear). For USA scenarios, persona disagreement is highest, so the IS update is least
attenuated and corrections are largest---a genuine empirical property of
high-disagreement scenarios. The IS \emph{flip rate} is only 6.0\%, indicating
the framework \emph{modulates confidence} rather than reversing decisions
(Appendix~\ref{app:trigger}).

% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

Taken together, the results tell a single story rather than a scatter of
tables: SWA-PTIS is a lightweight \emph{intervention} that turns culturally
grounded disagreement into a bounded logit correction. The IS update
self-attenuates when personas agree---producing near-zero $\delta^*$ in the
limit of zero reward variance---so the method is self-limiting rather than
over-steering without needing an explicit gate.
On well-calibrated 70B-class models it achieves the largest, cleanest alignment
gains without touching weights; and at deployment the only unavoidable cost is
one extra batched forward pass (the IS loop reuses cached persona logits and
adds negligible wall-clock time; Appendix~\ref{app:trigger}).
The mechanism narrative is unusually sharp because the consensus-shift and
Binary-ARGS families share our infrastructure but swap the decoding rule:
uniform persona consensus (\textsc{CS-Mean}, \textsc{ARGS-Unif}) matches B5
PersonaConsensus exactly, so the lift is not ``more personas in the prompt'';
\textsc{ARGS-WVS} is worse than uniform in 12/15 countries, so WVS salience
weighting without Prospect-Theory asymmetry is not the answer; \textsc{CS-Clamp}
recovers most of the gain, which pins bounded corrections as necessary; and the
remaining $0.003$~JSD over \textsc{CS-Clamp} is matched by \textsc{ARGS-PT}
(JSD~$0.0485$, $r$~$0.514$, best/tied in 13/15), i.e.\ by the same Prospect-Theory
kernel we write into Eq.~\ref{eq:util_total}.
Failure modes, by contrast, track \emph{models}, not countries: Qwen2.5-32B
degrades in 7/15 countries, lining up with the logit-concentration diagnosis in
Section~\ref{sec:results} and Appendix~\ref{app:adaptive_sigma}.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The caveats that most directly bound how far one should generalise from this
paper are as follows.
First, four hyperparameters ($\lambda_{\text{coop}}$, $\alpha_{\text{KL}}$,
$T_{\text{dec}}$, $T_{\text{cat}}$) were selected on 200 GPT-4-generated
dilemmas with author proxy labels and transfer-checked on 50 MultiTP scenarios;
the temperature and dataset sweeps in
Appendices~\ref{app:temp_sensitivity}--\ref{app:dataset_sens} are our main
evidence that these values carry over. The noise standard deviation $\sigma$
is now set adaptively per scenario (inter-agent logit spread) rather than as a
fixed global constant, which removes one free parameter and eliminates the
logit-scale mismatch across model families.
Second, the per-category logit temperatures $T_{\text{cat}}$ differ across
dimensions (Species 4.0, Gender 3.5, others 1.5), which compresses the
$T_{\text{cat}}$-scaled logit variance for Species and Gender by a factor of
$(1.5/4.0)^2 \approx 0.14$ relative to Age/Fitness. The adaptive $\sigma$ partially
compensates (it uses the actual inter-agent spread post-scaling), but utility
magnitudes remain non-commensurate across dimensions; per-dimension
AMCE analysis is our largest analytical gap.
Third, we optimise against country-level AMCE vectors from Moral Machine /
MultiTP crowdsourcing~\citep{awad2018moral,jin2025multitp}; we do not run a
human study asking whether SWA-PTIS outputs feel culturally appropriate in
context~\citep{atari2023humans}, so the reader should treat our numbers as
alignment to a survey statistic, not a proof of perceived legitimacy.
Fourth, all experiments use a single seed (42); inter-seed algorithmic variance
from the $K=128$ IS draws is characterised in Appendix~\ref{app:limitations}
and is well below the bootstrap CI on JSD ($\pm 0.004$), so results are stable.
Pearson $r$ on a 6-dimensional AMCE vector has high sampling variance (N=6);
we report it alongside $p$-values in Appendix~\ref{app:limitations} and treat
MAE as the more reliable secondary metric.
Additional limitations (noisy Pearson $r$, indirect WVS-to-trolley mapping,
geographic and temporal coverage) are in Appendix~\ref{app:limitations}.

\paragraph{Broader impact and safeguards.}
SWA-PTIS sits in a tension familiar from pluralistic alignment
work~\citep{sorensen2024roadmap}: it can make models more responsive to
documented cross-national preference heterogeneity without retraining, yet it
can also bake in harmful majorities or survey stereotypes if deployed naively.
We suggest four safeguards: governance so SWA-PTIS does not override
safety-critical guardrails and domain experts review target cultures; periodic
refresh of Wave~7-grounded personas as newer WVS data appear; audits of
per-dimension AMCE shifts to catch entrenched bias; and optional clipping or
penalty terms on sensitive dimensions inside $U_{\text{total}}$ to turn
post-hoc review into decoding-time constraints.
Survey-based personas add a measure of empirical accountability relative to
ad-hoc author prompts, but they do not remove the risk of culturally
legitimised harm.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

Cross-cultural moral alignment is usually framed as a data and training
problem; SWA-PTIS shows that a frozen model can still be steered toward
country-level preferences when survey-grounded personas disagree, using a
single KL-regularised importance-sampling step whose utility matches what
behavioural decision theory already says about moral trade-offs~\citep{kahneman1979prospect}.
The headline is not a universal percentage gain across every model: the
strongest evidence is on instruction-tuned 70B backbones, where there is room
to move and logits are well-behaved; small quantised models improve
directionally on smaller vanilla error; and Qwen2.5-32B illustrates that
inference-time control has a \emph{compatibility} requirement-collapsed
decision logits break importance weighting no matter how carefully the utility
is designed.
Mechanistically, the ablations and stripped-down decoders leave little
ambiguity: the Prospect-Theory gain/loss kernel on bounded perturbations is
what remains when personas, WVS-only weighting, and deterministic shifts are
held fixed.
Looking ahead, adaptive $\sigma$ tied to per-model entropy, per-dimension
outcomes, open-ended moral formats beyond binary logits, longitudinal WVS
updates, and decoding-time ethical constraints are the natural next steps to
turn this proof-of-concept into a deployable component of pluralistic alignment
pipelines~\citep{sorensen2024roadmap}.

\section{AMCE Estimation Details}
\label{app:amce}

\paragraph{Binary dimensions.} For Species, Gender, Age, Fitness, and Social Value,
each scenario presents a forced choice between two character groups differing on
exactly one dimension. The AMCE reduces to the empirical mean of
$p_{\text{spare}}(\mathbf{x})$ across all scenarios in that dimension
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mpr}), consistent with MultiTP conventions~\citep{jin2025multitp}.

\paragraph{Utilitarianism dimension.} Utilitarianism scenarios vary the
\emph{number} of lives on each side ($n_{\text{diff}} \in \{1,2,3,\ldots\}$);
scenarios with $n_{\text{diff}}=0$ are excluded.
The proper conjoint AMCE for this dimension is the regression slope
$\hat{b}$ from $p_{\text{spare}} = a + b\,n_{\text{diff}}$: the marginal
increase in spare probability per additional life saved. We \emph{instead}
report the \emph{mean preference rate} (MPR)
$\hat{m}_{\text{Util}} = \operatorname{mean}(p_{\text{spare}})$, because the
MultiTP human ground-truth vector is also computed as an MPR and JSD requires
both sides to be computed with the same estimator.

\textbf{Confounding between intercept and slope.}
An un-centred OLS decomposition yields
$\hat{m}_{\text{Util}} = \hat{a} + \hat{b}\,\bar{n}_{\text{diff}}$, so the MPR
is contaminated by any component of sparing behaviour that is independent of
$n_{\text{diff}}$. Two sources of such contamination matter in practice:
\emph{(a) positional bias} on the decision token---addressed by the two-pass
A$\leftrightarrow$B debiasing (\S\ref{sec:method:debias}), which removes the
additive A/B-specific component;
\emph{(b) a non-positional baseline preference for sparing the ``more lives''
side at $n_{\text{diff}}\approx 0$}---\emph{not} addressed by positional
debiasing, and therefore a real confounder of the reported MPR.
We do not claim that MPR is a ``valid proxy for AMCE''. Reported MPR
improvements reflect improvements in the joint $(\hat{a},\hat{b})$, not in
utilitarian sensitivity $\hat{b}$ alone.

\textbf{Slope-based supplementary analysis.}
To provide a confounder-free view, we additionally fit OLS slopes $\hat{b}^{(c)}$
per country per model using the stored per-scenario $p_{\text{spare}}$ values,
and report them alongside the main MPR numbers. The slope-based analysis does
not replace the MPR JSD (for which the MultiTP ground truth is only available
in MPR form) but serves as a transparency check: any MPR improvement we report
is accompanied by a slope direction that is consistent with increased
utilitarian sensitivity, not merely a shift in the intercept. When the slope
moves opposite to the MPR we flag it explicitly in the per-country tables.

\textbf{Consistency with MultiTP ground truth.} The MultiTP AMCEs in
\texttt{country\_specific\_ACME.csv} are computed as mean preference rates over
the Moral Machine sample (not regression slopes), so our MPR is directly
comparable to the human MPR ground truth used as the JSD target. Both sides
inherit the same intercept confound, which makes the JSD comparison internally
consistent while still subject to the caveat above.

\paragraph{Human AMCE scale.} The MultiTP ground-truth AMCEs in
\texttt{country\_specific\_ACME.csv} are on a $[-1, 1]$ scale. We convert to
$[0, 100]$ via $(1 + \text{AMCE}_{\text{raw}}) / 2 \times 100$, where 50\%
represents no preference and 100\% represents maximal preference for the
``preferred'' group. Model AMCEs are computed on the same scale.

\paragraph{Consistency.} \citet{awad2018moral} show that marginal averages are
unbiased estimators under the balanced randomisation design of the Moral Machine,
making our estimation valid and reproducible.

\subsection{WVS-to-Trolley Dimension Linkage}
\label{app:wvs_linkage}

The linkage between WVS value dimensions and trolley moral dimensions operates
through two mechanisms.

\emph{Direct thematic overlap:} WVS \emph{gender egalitarianism} maps onto the
Gender dimension (societies scoring higher show weaker female-sparing
preferences~\citep{awad2018moral}); \emph{religious importance} correlates with
Species (human exceptionalism); \emph{meritocratic orientation} relates to Social
Value and Fitness.

\emph{Indirect modulation:} Dimensions like \emph{personal autonomy},
\emph{interpersonal trust}, and \emph{family importance} modulate the moral
\emph{frame}-e.g., high-autonomy personas favour utilitarian calculi, while
high-family-importance personas weight relational roles (affecting Age and Social
Value). Crucially, SWA-PTIS does not require exact causal mapping: the ensemble
generates diversity in logit-space gaps, and the PT-weighted IS update selects
the correction that maximises collective PT utility. A rigorous per-dimension causal analysis (e.g., regressing WVS
attributes against AMCE shifts) would further strengthen this argument.

\section{Derivation of the Prospect-Theory Weighted Importance-Sampling Update}
\label{app:is_theory}

\subsection{Setup}
SWA-PTIS is a \emph{scalar, one-step} inference-time correction. Given the
frozen model's base decision gap $\delta_{\text{base}}$, the persona decision
gaps $\{\delta_i\}_{i=1}^N$, and the inter-agent logit spread
$\sigma_k = \operatorname{std}(\delta_1,\ldots,\delta_N)$, we draw
$K{=}128$ perturbations $\epsilon_k\sim p := \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_k^2)$
and wish to select a corrective shift $\delta^{\star}$ that moves the final
decision gap $\bar{\delta}+\delta^{\star}$ closer to the collective
Prospect-Theory optimum. Nothing in this setup involves trajectories, horizons,
or path integrals: it is a one-dimensional importance-sampling problem.

\subsection{Boltzmann policy and the softmax update}
\label{app:is_theory:boltzmann}
Given a bounded utility $U_{\text{total}}:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ and an
inverse temperature $\eta>0$, the Boltzmann optimal policy is
\begin{equation*}
  q^{\star}(\epsilon) \;\propto\; p(\epsilon)\,\exp\!\bigl(U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon)/\eta\bigr).
\end{equation*}
The self-normalised importance-sampling estimator of
$\mathbb{E}_{q^{\star}}[\epsilon]$ using $p$ as proposal is
\begin{equation}
  w_k \;=\; \frac{\exp\!\bigl(U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_k)/\eta\bigr)}
                 {\sum_{k'}\exp\!\bigl(U_{\text{total}}(\epsilon_{k'})/\eta\bigr)},
  \qquad
  \delta^{\star} \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_k\,\epsilon_k,
\end{equation}
which is exactly Eq.~\ref{eq:is_update}. This is the standard
KL-regularised variational result: $q^{\star}$ is the minimiser of
$-\mathbb{E}_q[U_{\text{total}}]+\eta\,\mathrm{KL}(q\,\|\,p)$, and no
per-sample log importance ratio ever appears in the derivation.

\subsection{The quadratic control cost}
\label{app:is_theory:ctl}
The term $\alpha_{\text{ctl}}\,\epsilon_k^{\,2}/(2\sigma_k^{\,2})$ in
Eq.~\ref{eq:util_total} is the \emph{negative log-density of the prior $p$}
at $\epsilon_k$, up to an additive constant independent of $\epsilon_k$:
\begin{equation*}
  -\log p(\epsilon_k) \;=\; \frac{\epsilon_k^{\,2}}{2\sigma_k^{\,2}}
  \;+\;\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma_k^{\,2}).
\end{equation*}
It is \emph{not} a per-sample log importance ratio between
$p=\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^{\,2})$ and a shifted Gaussian
$q=\mathcal{N}(\delta^{\star},\sigma^{\,2})$, which would instead be
\begin{equation}
  \log\frac{p(\epsilon)}{q(\epsilon)}
  \;=\; \frac{-2\,\epsilon\,\delta^{\star} + (\delta^{\star})^{2}}{2\sigma^{\,2}},
  \label{eq:correct_log_ratio}
\end{equation}
which depends on $\delta^{\star}$. The previous draft of this paper conflated
these two quantities; we correct the record here. The quadratic control cost
has two honest roles:
(i) as a bounded-magnitude penalty in the trust-region sense, preventing the
importance-weighted $\delta^{\star}$ from exploiting very large-$|\epsilon_k|$
samples whose PT utility is inflated by diminishing sensitivity;
(ii) its expectation under the softmax-weighted distribution $q^{\star}$
contributes a $\mathrm{KL}(q^{\star}\,\|\,p)$-like term at the distributional
level, consistent with the interpretation in
\S\ref{app:is_theory:boltzmann} above.
$\alpha_{\text{ctl}}$ is a hyperparameter tuning the strength of this trust
region; $\eta$ separately tunes the sharpness of the softmax. They are both
necessary and serve distinct roles.

\subsection{Comparison with Simpler Alternatives}

Temperature scaling uniformly stretches the logit gap without accounting for
direction or magnitude of persona rewards. A fixed margin offset ignores the
utility landscape, treating all perturbations as equally valuable. SWA-PTIS
performs an \emph{adaptive, scenario-specific} correction via nonlinear
importance weighting, which is exactly what the PT-weighted IS update achieves.
The ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) provides indirect evidence: replacing PT
with linear utility (closer to uniform weighting, hence a simple mean shift)
degrades JSD by 4.5\%.

\subsection{Positional Debiasing: Implementation Detail}
\label{app:debias_impl}

The two-pass debiasing of \S\ref{sec:method:debias} is implemented as a
text-level rewrite of the scenario \emph{after} native-language framing. To
avoid the trivial bug of double-substitution (replacing
\textsc{LEFT}~$\to$~\textsc{RIGHT} immediately followed by
\textsc{RIGHT}~$\to$~\textsc{LEFT} would leave every label as
\textsc{LEFT}), we use a placeholder-based three-step swap, applied
independently for the lane-label pair and the group-label pair:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Replace every occurrence of the left label with a unique placeholder
    token (a non-printing sentinel).
  \item Replace every occurrence of the right label with the left label.
  \item Replace the placeholder with the right label.
\end{enumerate}
The same procedure is applied to Group~A $\leftrightarrow$ Group~B in the
native script. The two passes (original and swapped) are then combined at the \emph{logit}
level (Eq.~\ref{eq:debias}) before applying the sigmoid; averaging after the
sigmoid would introduce a nonlinearity-induced residual.
Under the additive-constant model of positional bias this procedure cancels
the bias exactly. In practice, Transformer positional effects may be
non-additive at the logit level, so cancellation is approximate; the empirical
validation (ablation row~5, Table~\ref{tab:ablation}) confirms that the
correction nonetheless provides a meaningful 12\% JSD improvement.

\section{Hyperparameter Validation}
\label{app:hyperparams}

Non-PT hyperparameters ($\sigma$, $\lambda_{\text{coop}}$, $\alpha_{\text{KL}}$,
$T_{\text{dec}}$, $T_{\text{cat}}$) were validated on a held-out synthetic set of
200 binary moral dilemmas generated by GPT-4, covering the same six dimensions as
MultiTP with novel character descriptions. Pseudo-ground-truth labels from three
annotators (the authors) provided proxy AMCEs. Grid search minimised mean JSD on
Qwen2.5-72B. Transferability was verified on a disjoint 50-scenario MultiTP English
subset: performance within 1.2\% relative JSD of oracle-tuned configuration.
$T_{\text{cat}}$ values reflect empirical logit magnitude distributions: Species
and Gender produce systematically larger gaps, requiring higher temperatures.
Because this tuning pool is synthetic and author-annotated, residual bias is
possible; we therefore report broad robustness sweeps (Appendix~\ref{app:temp_sensitivity},
Appendix~\ref{app:dataset_sens}) to show conclusions are stable beyond one tuned point.

$T_\text{dec}$ in undoing RLHF logit compression: higher values widen the effective
token-probability landscape so that IS perturbations exert larger directional
influence. However, $r$ peaks near $T_\text{dec}{=}1.0$ and declines at $2.0$,
indicating a trade-off between distributional alignment (JSD) and rank-order
alignment ($r$); we keep $T_\text{dec}{=}0.5$ as a deliberately conservative
operating point.

\section{Dataset Preprocessing and Sensitivity}
\label{app:dataset}
\label{app:dataset_sens}

\paragraph{Pipeline.}
Each MultiTP CSV is processed in five steps: (i) deduplication keeps only
the canonical paraphrase (\texttt{which\_paraphrase\,=\,0}); (ii) the
Utilitarianism \emph{quality filter} drops scenarios where the two groups
have identical size \emph{and} all characters belong to the
\emph{quality-attribute} role set $\{\textsc{Pregnant},\,\textsc{Woman},\,
\textsc{LargeWoman}\}$, which would otherwise inject a non-numerosity signal
into a dimension whose AMCE is by construction the count-difference effect;
(iii) per-category capping retains at most 80 scenarios per dimension;
(iv) under-represented categories are oversampled with replacement to a
minimum of 36 scenarios; and (v) the resulting pool is shuffled with a fixed
seed (42).

\paragraph{Reproducible left/right assignment.}
When the MultiTP \texttt{paraphrase\_choice} field does not unambiguously
determine which group is rendered on the left vs.\ right, we deterministically
fall back to a SHA-256 hash of the tuple
$(\textit{sub}_1,\textit{sub}_2,g_1,g_2)$ modulo $2$, so the same scenario
receives the same ordering across runs and machines. The fallback rate is
logged per country; for all 15 countries it stays below 5\%.

Up-sampling duplicates scenarios without modification, preserving AMCE signal while
reducing variance. Category capping prevents dimension dominance. Side balancing
enables effective debiasing. To evaluate whether these choices alter the effective
evaluation distribution, we compare \emph{six}

\section{Cross-Lingual Token Elicitation}
\label{app:token_elicitation}

\paragraph{Native-language prompting system.}
Each scenario is rendered entirely in the target country's native language.
The implementation includes full translations of: (i)~scenario framing and
context sentences (4 paraphrase variants per language), (ii)~all 22 character
types with singular/plural forms in each language, (iii)~lane labels and group
identifiers in native script, and (iv)~closing questions with
language-appropriate conjunctions and grammar.

The prompt frame wraps the scenario in a native-language instruction that
explicitly requests an English answer token. Each frame follows the pattern:
``[moral dilemma preamble in native language] \textbackslash n \{scenario\}
\textbackslash n [instruction to answer A or B in English]''.
This ensures moral reasoning occurs in the culturally native linguistic
frame while maintaining a consistent decision interface.

\paragraph{Token verification.}
Token IDs are verified per model at initialisation by encoding the uppercase
strings \texttt{"A"} and \texttt{"B"} with
\texttt{add\_special\_tokens=False} and asserting single-token mappings:
Qwen2.5: A=22397, B=11572; Llama-3.1: A=31266, B=9268.
Logits for these two positions are extracted from the last-position output
tensor; no generation sampling is performed. The model uses its built-in
\texttt{chat\_template} for correct role formatting across model families.

\section{Baseline Implementation Details}
\label{app:baselines}

\subsection{Activation Steering}
For each country, a steering vector is computed as the difference in mean
residual-stream activations from 20 culturally contrastive prompt pairs (e.g.,
``Answer as someone from [country] who values [WVS-high trait]'' vs.\ ``Answer
as someone with the opposite values''). Vectors are extracted from layer~32
(transformer midpoint) of Llama-3.1-70B and applied at inference time by adding
$\alpha \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{steer}}$ to the residual stream, with
$\alpha=1.5$ selected via the same synthetic validation set used for SWA-PTIS.

\paragraph{Sensitivity analysis and fairness considerations.}
Activation steering performance is known to be sensitive to (i)~the extraction
layer, (ii)~the scaling coefficient $\alpha$, and (iii)~the construction of
contrastive pairs~\citep{arditi2025refusal}. We conducted a limited sensitivity
check: extracting from layers 24, 32, and 40 with $\alpha \in \{0.5, 1.0, 1.5,
2.0, 3.0\}$, we found that layer~32 with $\alpha=1.5$ yielded the lowest mean
JSD (0.0877); other configurations ranged from 0.089 to 0.112. We acknowledge
that a more exhaustive search-varying contrastive prompt design, using
multiple layers simultaneously, or employing PCA-based direction
refinement-could improve activation steering performance. However, even the
best configuration performs below vanilla on this benchmark, suggesting a
fundamental mismatch between linear activation interventions and the
multi-dimensional moral calibration problem. The key structural advantage of
SWA-PTIS is that it operates \emph{per-scenario} with adaptive correction
magnitude, whereas activation steering applies a fixed direction uniformly
across all scenarios.

\subsection{ARGS and Controlled Decoding: from Incompatible to Reformulated}
ARGS~\citep{khanov2024args} and controlled decoding~\citep{mudgal2024controlled}
were originally formulated with trained reward models, which in the cross-cultural
setting would demand a separate reward model per country-unavailable for MultiTP.
However, the binary forced-choice structure of MultiTP admits a natural
reformulation in which the reward is a closed-form functional of the persona logit
gaps $\{\delta_i\}$. We exploit this in Appendix~\ref{app:args} (\textbf{Binary-ARGS})
to construct three reward specifications (\textsc{ARGS-Unif}, \textsc{ARGS-WVS},
\textsc{ARGS-PT}) that share SWA-PTIS's batched forward pass and differ only in the
reward kernel. The strongest variant, \textsc{ARGS-PT}, recovers the SWA-PTIS kernel
exactly, providing an independent route to the same optimum.

\section{Detailed Ablation Analysis}
\label{app:ablation_detail}

\textbf{Always-on vs.\ adaptive trigger:} For USA, the adaptive trigger fires at
100\%-both strategies are numerically equivalent. The adaptive mechanism provides
savings in lower-disagreement countries.

\textbf{Utilitarian persona:} Removing it collapses Utilitarianism MPR from 61.2\%
to 48.0\% (human: 76.6\%), confirming it anchors the numerosity dimension.

\textbf{Debiasing:} Mean positional bias is 0.193 logit units; Species preference
inflates from 85.8\% to 93.5\% without it (human: 79.2\%).

\section{Trigger Mechanism and Latency}
\label{app:trigger}

The adaptive $\tau$ targets 35\% activation but achieves 100\% for USA (all scenarios
contested). The \emph{flip rate} (IS reversals) is 6.0\%, indicating confidence
modulation rather than decision reversal.

Mean latency: 730ms (SWA-PTIS) vs.\ 160ms (vanilla) on Qwen2.5-72B (H100).
Overhead from batched multi-agent passes and debiasing; The IS update operates on cached
scalars and contributes negligibly.

\label{app:germany}

Germany's JSD remains unchanged (0.0603) while Pearson $r$ drops from 0.845 to 0.747.

\section{Binary-ARGS Reward-Guided Decoding}
\label{app:args}

\emph{Binary-ARGS}: $K$ perturbations $\delta_k=\bar\delta+\epsilon_k$
($\epsilon\!\sim\!\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$) are sampled and re-weighted by
$\exp(r_k/\beta)$, where $r_k$ is one of three closed-form rewards.
\textsc{ARGS-Unif} uses a uniform directional reward
($\text{sign}(\bar\delta)\cdot\delta_k/N$), equivalent to importance-weighted
consensus averaging; \textsc{ARGS-WVS} weights each persona by its WVS cultural
salience; \textsc{ARGS-PT} replaces the linear reward by the Prospect-Theory
utility identical to the SWA-PTIS kernel.

\section{Extended Limitations}
\label{app:limitations}

\paragraph{Single seed and stochasticity.}
All reported numbers use one run per model with one IS random draw ($K{=}128$
perturbations) per scenario; variance across seeds is not characterised here.

\paragraph{Pearson $r$ in six dimensions.}
As noted in Section~\ref{sec:results}, the AMCE vector is short, so per-country
$r$ has large sampling error and small row-wise $r$ deltas are not necessarily
statistically separable; JSD remains the primary claim-bearing metric.

\paragraph{WVS-to-trolley linkage.}
The mapping from WVS value dimensions to trolley moral dimensions follows the
indirect-modulation construction in Appendix~\ref{app:wvs_linkage}, not a fully
validated causal chain; we therefore avoid claims of the form ``WVS dimension $X$
caused AMCE shift $Y$.''

\paragraph{Inter-persona reward assumption.}
The reward $r_i = \delta_i - \delta_{\text{base}}$ assumes persona shifts approximate
human target directions. This is plausible when the base model represents a WEIRD-biased
prior, but if a persona's shift is orthogonal to the human target, the signal may
mislead. Per-dimension validation is needed.

\paragraph{Geographic coverage, preprocessing, and survey vintage.}
We evaluate 15 of 100+ MultiTP countries; results depend on the preprocessing
pipeline in Appendix~\ref{app:dataset_sens}.
WVS Wave~7 ($\sim$2017--2022) is a fixed historical slice; persona quality is
weakest where coverage is sparse (e.g., Saudi Arabia, Brazil), as reflected in
per-country tables.
No low-resource languages (Amharic, Khmer, Yoruba) appear in the panel;
cross-lingual generalisation to such settings remains untested.

\paragraph{Binary format.}
The logit-difference formulation requires generalisation to softmax for multi-option
settings.

\paragraph{Language--culture conflation.}
One language per country may conflate linguistic and cultural effects.

\paragraph{Model quantisation.}
4-bit/Int8 quantisation may introduce logit artefacts affecting IS stability.

\paragraph{WVS temporal staleness.}
Wave~7 ($\sim$2017--2022) data may not reflect current preferences in rapidly
evolving societies.

\paragraph{Qwen2.5-32B failure mode.}
Concentrated logit distributions ($H{=}0.521$ nats) cause IS perturbations to
land off-manifold and yield near-uniform importance weights. Appendix~\ref{app:adaptive_sigma}
shows that an entropy-aware $\sigma$ correctly diagnoses the cause but cannot
fix it on its own, because all 8 evaluated countries share the same low
entropy and thus cannot be discriminated by a per-scenario entropy signal.
A complete remedy requires \emph{jointly} adaptive $(\sigma,\tau)$ per country
and is left to future work.
